In older TLS certificates, browsers checked the Common Name (CN) to verify the website name. If the URL was example.com, the certificate needed CN=example.com.

This was limited because CN could contain only one name. The Subject Alternative Name (SAN) extension was created to allow multiple domains or IP addresses in one certificate.

Modern browsers such as Mozilla Firefox now ignore CN and check SAN only, because SAN is the standard and avoids ambiguity.

To fix the problem when using OpenSSL, generate the certificate with a SAN field that includes the hostname you access. If the hostname appears in SAN and the CA is trusted, the browser will accept the certificate (details in the OPENSSL Script).
